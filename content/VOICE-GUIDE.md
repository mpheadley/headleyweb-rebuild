# Voice Guide: Headley Web & SEO

*Instructions for Claude when drafting, editing, or reviewing any copy on headleyweb.com.*

**This guide applies to all site copy** — homepage, services, about, FAQ, CTAs, meta descriptions, JSON-LD text, and blog posts. If it's on the site and a human can read it, run it through the kill list and the brochure test.

---

## The Standard

**Voice differentiates.** If a potential client reads your site and it sounds like every other agency, you're invisible. If it sounds like the guy who talked to a woodworker at a kid's birthday party and built him a site, that sticks.

**Authenticity bar is non-negotiable.** The craft level varies by context (a blog post gets more room than a CTA button), but nothing ships that sounds like it was generated.

**Feed Matt's voice back to him.** Before drafting anything, read copy Matt has already approved. Match that register — the specific references, the sentence length, the way he doesn't over-explain.

---

## The Kill List

### Hard List — Never Use

**Verbs:** delve, foster, leverage, harness, underscore, showcase, navigate (metaphorically), bolster, streamline, unlock, unleash, elevate, align, empower, revolutionize, supercharge, transform

**Permitted:** "optimize" and "optimization" are OK — standard SEO/web terminology, not brochure fluff.

**Adjectives:** robust, pivotal, seamless, multifaceted, cutting-edge

**Phrases:**
- "In today's fast-paced world..."
- "It's worth noting that..."
- "The intersection of X and Y"
- "A tapestry of..."
- "From X to Y, ..."
- "Whether you're a...or a..."
- "In an era of..."
- "This is not just about X, it's about Y"
- "Moreover" / "Furthermore" / "Additionally" (as paragraph openers)
- "In conclusion"
- "Let's dive in" / "Let's explore"
- "Tailored solutions" / "unique needs" / "seamless experience"
- "In the digital age" / "digital landscape"
- Any sentence that starts: "As a business owner, you know that..."

**Punctuation:** Em dashes. Semicolons in conversational prose.

### Use Sparingly

*vital, crucial, nuanced, vibrant, innovative, intricate, unprecedented, comprehensive, dynamic, enhance, highlight, notably*

## The Brochure Failure Mode

Web design copy defaults to brochure voice. Adjectives doing the work that evidence should do.

> *Brochure:* "We build beautiful, high-performing websites tailored to your unique business goals, with a seamless user experience that converts visitors into loyal customers."

> *Real:* "I build websites for local businesses in Northeast Alabama. Most of my clients were either invisible on Google or paying for a site that looked fine but didn't do anything. I fix that."

**The test:** Is this sentence making a claim or showing evidence? Claims without evidence are brochure. Replace with something specific: a number, a place, a real client situation (without naming them), a problem you've actually solved.

---

## Structural Patterns to Break

**Hedging reflex.** "It could be argued..." / "Many businesses find that..." If it's true, say it. If it isn't, don't write it.

**Tricolon reflex.** AI loves groups of three. Use two. Or four. Or one.

**Paragraph uniformity.** Vary the lengths. A one-sentence paragraph has more impact than a five-sentence paragraph that contains the same idea.

**Summary conclusion.** Don't end by restating what you just said. End with the next thing the reader should do, or one sentence that makes the whole piece land. Then stop.

## Site Copy Rules

**Source of truth:** Pull all copy from `website-copy.md`. Don't invent copy during the build.

**FAQ and JSON-LD:** Visible FAQ text and structured data must match exactly. When editing one, always edit the other.

**CTAs:** Short. Direct. No brochure adjectives. "Get a free site checkup" beats "Discover how we can transform your online presence."

**Pain points and consequences:** Use specifics, not fear-mongering generalities. "Your competitor shows up first on Google Maps" beats "You're falling behind in the digital landscape."

## Blog Post Rules

### What These Posts Are

SEO-first, 400-500 words. Someone landed here from a Google search looking for an answer. The job is to give them the answer in a voice that makes them trust you enough to click through to services.

Not literary journalism. Not a case study. Not a brochure. A real person thinking on the page about something they know well.

### Openings

The opening decides whether they stay. Two sentences. It has to do one of these:
- Name a specific problem the reader came here with
- Say something true that most web design blogs wouldn't say
- Drop a specific detail that signals you're not generic

> *Generic:* "In today's digital landscape, having a strong online presence is more important than ever."
> *Real:* "Most small business websites don't rank on Google because they were built to look good in a demo, not to answer the questions people are actually typing."

If the opening could have been written by any web agency anywhere, rewrite it.

### TLDRs

Keep them on every post. Google AI Overviews and featured snippets pull from concise summary text. Format:

```
tldr: "One sentence that answers the post's core question plainly."
```

Write it last. If you can't summarize the post in one sentence, the post doesn't have a clear point yet.

## Self-Audit Checklist

Run before returning any draft or edit to Matt.

### All copy (site + blog)
- [ ] Kill-list words present? (search: leverage, seamless, showcase, robust, tapestry, delve)
- [ ] Em dashes? Replace with period, comma, or rephrase.
- [ ] Semicolons in conversational prose? Rewrite as two sentences.
- [ ] Does every claim have evidence, or is it brochure?
- [ ] Tricolon groups? Break the pattern.
- [ ] Could a competitor's site say the same thing with their name swapped in? Make it specific.

### Blog posts only
- [ ] Opening: could it have been written by any web agency? Rewrite it.
- [ ] Does it end with a summary restatement? Cut it.
- [ ] Paragraph heights all the same? Break up uniformity.
- [ ] TLDR present and one sentence?
- [ ] Word count 400-500?
