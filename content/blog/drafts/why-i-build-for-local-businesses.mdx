## DRAFT — Option A: Personal / Emotional angle

### Hook

You've spent 19 years working hard — pastoral ministry, running a flower farm, providing for your family. You know what it takes to grind. But the hardest season wasn't any of those.

### The Breaking Point

Reference the personal challenge without oversharing. Keep it one paragraph. The point isn't the details — it's what came out the other side. Something like:

> "A few years ago, I went through something that broke me down in a way I didn't think I'd come back from. I'm not going to give you the whole story here — that's not what this is about. But what came out of it was a version of me that doesn't quit. Not on a hard day. Not on a hard project. Not on a client who's counting on me."

### The Realization

Coming out the other side, you noticed the same thing you wrote about in your first blog post — great local businesses, invisible online. But now you had something different: a resilience that meant you'd see every project through, no matter what.

### Why Local

You're not building for tech startups or national brands. You're building for the contractor who's too busy to think about SEO. The HVAC tech whose competitor just started showing up in the map pack. The small business owner who's *great at what they do* but doesn't know how to make Google agree. These are your neighbors. You get it because you've been one.

### What Resilience Looks Like in Practice

This is where you connect the personal story to the client outcome. 19 years of hard work + surviving the hardest season of your life = you don't cut corners, you don't ghost, you don't ship something half-finished. Every site gets the same persistence that got you here.
